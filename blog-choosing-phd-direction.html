choosing my phd direction

19 May 2026

If I try to evaluate my trajectory in research, I feel I jumped on the next available opportunity without really thinking about why I am doing this and how it aligns with my larger goal. But then, what was my goal? Or more like, what IS my goal? I really do not know. There are infinite pathways I can take, and still feel fulfilled in 90% of those. If that is the case, then maybe I should pick one and let the rest be in the hands of Allah. Of course, I will still put some thought into it. I remember reading some words of Yasser Bashir1 who said that 90% of the decisions you take are the right decisions. 10% are those which are salvageable if they go bad. So, maybe I should really not think super deep into it and just make a decision. But then, there is one part of me who feels this decision will inform the trajectory of my future life. Whatever trajectory I choose, I will most likely be required by circumstances to stick with it. So maybe this is why I want to not overthink about my decision and worry about my future, but still think enough to satisfy what I am doing that seems to not go off rails later.

This is why maybe I should start with my values. I really like it when I work on digital safety. I like the part of policy in it where I am advocating for people by telling those big tech firms and governments what they are doing wrong and how to improve it. All of my pre-CISPA projects are in this domain and while they were not really planned, I loved their outputs. I loved talking to people, finding out their pain points, figuring out what is wrong in the system and presenting it to the world as an advocate. Advocacy. Maybe that is something which is one of my values. I like to work for the good of people by advocating for their rights. This kind of ties to Henrik's idea of taking risks to stay interesting.2 While I risk my visa, family protection, my safety, and career for this kind of work, it helps me stay grounded.

Let's think of policy and advocacy more. There was a time in my life when I wanted to do Central Superior Services (CSS), a Pakistani civil service exam, and serve the public. Then I felt like contributing to the GDPR and AI policies. And then the social media blocking in Australia came in which got me excited. It seems super exciting to me. Even now. I want to do policy work. But there are not many jobs in policy in foreign governments and in Pakistan I would ideally want to do that through Bilal bin Saqib's3 route. With that being said, I feel I would not die of hunger. I would be doing something meaningful while maybe not earning as much. But then money is something I cannot control.4 In light of these layoffs, I do not think chasing money is going to get me anywhere. Maybe these are justifications I have created for myself to satisfy the side of my brain and heart which wants to do what triggers dopamine. I do not know. But then, nice line of argument anyway.

I also want to stay market relevant. I want to be in control of my work. I want to do the kind of work which cannot be replaced by AI. More like, the work which cannot be automated and remains relevant. Lots of tangents in the past few sentences, but well it is a brain dump anyway. So, I want to do work which remains industry relevant, cannot be automated, be more towards advocacy, help people, earn me suitable sums of money, and what i can easily control. Phew. The authentication work I did kind of does all of this. While it is not as advocacy heavy as I would ideally want it to be, it still checks all the boxes. I liked that work primarily because of its methodology. I love developing methods and I feel this kind of work cannot be automated at all. I aspire to do projects which are methods-heavy and help me grow by being creative with how I study the problems. Methods help me avoid mode-collapse. It helps me keep doing interesting stuff, take risks, and do stuff meaningful for researchers and the public alike. It, in some way, also does advocacy by calling out the bad in the research community and providing tools which can help it to study more and more populations and problems.

This is exactly where I find fulfillment in the survey study I am doing. It is a pain in the ass, but still something that can stir a revolution in the research community. In summary, I like to do work which is method-heavy, has an advocacy element in it, and is something I am proud of solving.

This brings me to the question: "What is the problem you should be working on now?"
